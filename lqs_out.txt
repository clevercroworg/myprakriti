Life Quality Score Assessment
Clinical Dietitian Edition — Chronic Condition Patients
Holistic assessment across 5 evidence-based domains
About This Assessment
This Life Quality Score (LQS) Assessment has been designed for clinical dietitians working with patients managing chronic conditions including Type 2 Diabetes, Obesity, Metabolic Syndrome, Hypertension, and related comorbidities.
The assessment covers 5 domains: Diet & Nutrition, Physical Activity, Sleep Quality, Stress & Mental Well-being, and Emotional Health. Each domain contains 5 questions with 4 scored response options.
Each question is scored 1–4, where 1 = most concerning and 4 = optimal. Domain scores and an overall Life Quality Score are calculated as described in the Scoring Guide section.
🥦  Domain 1: Diet & Nutrition
Q1.  How many servings of vegetables and fruits do you consume daily? (1 serving = 1 cup raw or half cup cooked)
  Score 1:  0–1 servings — rarely eat vegetables or fruits
  Score 2:  2–3 servings — occasional vegetables or fruits
  Score 3:  4–5 servings — regular intake most days
  Score 4:  6+ servings — consistently high plant food intake
Clinical note: Low vegetable intake is a primary risk factor for poor glycaemic control in Type 2 Diabetes and contributes to inflammation in obesity.
Q2.  How often do you consume ultra-processed foods? (fast food, packaged snacks, sweetened beverages, instant noodles)
  Score 1:  Daily — multiple times per day
  Score 2:  Most days — at least once per day
  Score 3:  Occasionally — 2–3 times per week
  Score 4:  Rarely or never — less than once per week
Clinical note: Ultra-processed foods are strongly associated with insulin resistance, dyslipidaemia, and weight gain in chronic condition patients.
Q3.  How often do you eat home-cooked meals versus restaurant or takeaway food?
  Score 1:  Almost always eating out or ordering in
  Score 2:  More often outside than home-cooked
  Score 3:  More often home-cooked than outside
  Score 4:  Almost always home-cooked meals
Clinical note: Home cooking allows better portion control and ingredient quality, both critical for managing diabetes and obesity.
Q4.  How many glasses of plain water do you drink daily? (exclude tea, coffee, juices)
  Score 1:  Less than 4 glasses — severely under-hydrated
  Score 2:  4–6 glasses — below recommended intake
  Score 3:  7–8 glasses — near adequate hydration
  Score 4:  8+ glasses — well-hydrated consistently
Clinical note: Adequate hydration supports kidney function, blood glucose regulation, and metabolism — especially critical in diabetes and obesity.
Q5.  How would you describe your meal timing and eating pattern throughout the day?
  Score 1:  Irregular — often skip meals or binge at night
  Score 2:  Inconsistent — miss 1–2 meals regularly
  Score 3:  Mostly regular — occasional missed meals
  Score 4:  Very regular — consistent meal times every day
Clinical note: Irregular meal patterns cause blood sugar fluctuations and disrupt hunger hormones (ghrelin/leptin), worsening chronic condition outcomes.
🏃  Domain 2: Physical Activity
Q1.  How many days per week do you engage in at least 30 minutes of moderate physical activity? (brisk walking, cycling, swimming)
  Score 1:  0 days — completely sedentary
  Score 2:  1–2 days — low activity level
  Score 3:  3–4 days — moderate activity level
  Score 4:  5–7 days — high activity level
Clinical note: WHO recommends 150 min/week moderate activity for adults with chronic conditions. Even 10-min walks after meals improve post-meal blood glucose.
Q2.  How long do you typically sit or remain sedentary without movement during a typical day?
  Score 1:  8+ hours of continuous sitting
  Score 2:  6–8 hours with minimal movement breaks
  Score 3:  4–6 hours with some short movement breaks
  Score 4:  Less than 4 hours — regularly break up sitting
Clinical note: Prolonged sitting independently increases insulin resistance and cardiovascular risk, even in physically active individuals.
Q3.  Do you engage in any strength or resistance training? (weights, resistance bands, bodyweight exercises)
  Score 1:  Never — no resistance training at all
  Score 2:  Rarely — once a month or less
  Score 3:  Sometimes — 1–2 times per week
  Score 4:  Regularly — 2–3 times per week or more
Clinical note: Resistance training builds muscle mass, which is the primary site of glucose disposal — directly improving insulin sensitivity in T2DM.
Q4.  How do you typically get around for short distances? (less than 1 km)
  Score 1:  Always use vehicle — never walk
  Score 2:  Usually drive — walk only if necessary
  Score 3:  Mix of walking and vehicle
  Score 4:  Walk or cycle for most short distances
Clinical note: Incidental walking (non-exercise activity thermogenesis / NEAT) contributes significantly to daily calorie expenditure and metabolic health.
Q5.  How would you describe your overall physical energy and stamina for daily tasks?
  Score 1:  Very low — exhausted by minor tasks
  Score 2:  Low — tire quickly with moderate effort
  Score 3:  Moderate — manage most tasks with some fatigue
  Score 4:  Good — handle daily tasks comfortably
Clinical note: Persistent fatigue in chronic condition patients may indicate poor metabolic control, anaemia, or deconditioning — all addressable through nutrition.
😴  Domain 3: Sleep Quality
Q1.  How many hours of sleep do you typically get on weeknights?
  Score 1:  Less than 5 hours — severely sleep-deprived
  Score 2:  5–6 hours — below recommended
  Score 3:  7–8 hours — within recommended range
  Score 4:  8–9 hours — adequate to optimal sleep
Clinical note: Sleep deprivation elevates cortisol and ghrelin, disrupts insulin sensitivity, and increases appetite for high-carb foods — directly worsening diabetes and obesity.
Q2.  How often do you wake up during the night and have difficulty going back to sleep?
  Score 1:  Almost every night — severe sleep disruption
  Score 2:  Several nights per week — frequent disruption
  Score 3:  Occasionally — once or twice a week
  Score 4:  Rarely — sleep through most nights
Clinical note: Fragmented sleep reduces deep (slow-wave) sleep, which is when growth hormone is secreted and cellular repair occurs.
Q3.  How often do you wake up in the morning feeling refreshed and rested?
  Score 1:  Almost never — always wake up tired
  Score 2:  Rarely — most mornings feel unrefreshed
  Score 3:  Sometimes — feel rested about half the time
  Score 4:  Most mornings — usually wake up refreshed
Clinical note: Unrefreshing sleep may indicate obstructive sleep apnoea, which is common in obesity and strongly linked to insulin resistance.
Q4.  What time do you typically go to bed on weeknights?
  Score 1:  After 1:00 AM — very late bedtime
  Score 2:  Between 12:00 AM and 1:00 AM
  Score 3:  Between 10:30 PM and 12:00 AM
  Score 4:  Before 10:30 PM — early, consistent bedtime
Clinical note: Late chronotype (sleeping late) is associated with higher BMI, worse glycaemic control, and higher HbA1c in T2DM patients.
Q5.  How often do you use screens (phone, TV, laptop) within 30 minutes of going to bed?
  Score 1:  Every night — always use screens at bedtime
  Score 2:  Most nights — 5–6 nights per week
  Score 3:  Occasionally — 2–3 nights per week
  Score 4:  Rarely — almost never use screens before bed
Clinical note: Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production, delaying sleep onset and reducing sleep quality — especially disruptive in patients with neuropathy or pain.
🧘  Domain 4: Stress & Mental Well-being
Q1.  How often do you feel overwhelmed, anxious, or unable to cope with your daily responsibilities?
  Score 1:  Almost daily — constantly overwhelmed
  Score 2:  Several times per week — frequent stress
  Score 3:  Occasionally — once or twice a week
  Score 4:  Rarely — generally feel in control
Clinical note: Chronic stress triggers cortisol release, which raises blood glucose, promotes visceral fat accumulation, and accelerates insulin resistance.
Q2.  Do you have any regular stress-management practices? (deep breathing, meditation, prayer, journaling, hobby)
  Score 1:  None — no stress-relief practices at all
  Score 2:  Rarely — only in crisis situations
  Score 3:  Sometimes — a few times a week
  Score 4:  Daily — consistent stress-relief routine
Clinical note: Even 5–10 minutes of mindfulness daily has been shown to lower HbA1c and improve dietary adherence in patients with T2DM.
Q3.  How often does work, family, or financial stress lead you to eat unhealthy foods or overeat?
  Score 1:  Very often — stress eating is a major issue
  Score 2:  Often — eat unhealthy when stressed
  Score 3:  Sometimes — occasional stress eating
  Score 4:  Rarely — stress rarely affects eating
Clinical note: Emotional/stress eating is a key behaviour pattern in obesity. Addressing it through nutrition counselling significantly improves long-term weight outcomes.
Q4.  How supported do you feel by family or friends in managing your health condition?
  Score 1:  Not at all supported — facing it alone
  Score 2:  Minimal support — rarely get encouragement
  Score 3:  Some support — occasional help and understanding
  Score 4:  Well supported — family actively helps
Clinical note: Social support is one of the strongest predictors of dietary adherence and medication compliance in patients with chronic conditions.
Q5.  How often do you feel hopeful or positive about your ability to manage and improve your health condition?
  Score 1:  Rarely — feel hopeless about my condition
  Score 2:  Sometimes — mixed feelings about outcomes
  Score 3:  Often — generally positive with some doubt
  Score 4:  Almost always — confident and motivated
Clinical note: Self-efficacy and positive outlook are strongly predictive of treatment adherence and health outcomes in both T2DM and obesity management.
💚  Domain 5: Emotional Health
Q1.  How would you describe your overall relationship with food and eating on a daily basis?
  Score 1:  Very stressful — eating causes guilt and anxiety
  Score 2:  Complicated — food is often a source of shame
  Score 3:  Neutral — mostly eat without strong emotions
  Score 4:  Positive — enjoy eating mindfully and guilt-free
Clinical note: A negative relationship with food is associated with binge-eating disorder, yo-yo dieting, and poor long-term weight management in obese patients.
Q2.  How often do you experience positive emotions — such as joy, contentment, gratitude, or connection — during your week?
  Score 1:  Rarely — most days feel heavy or joyless
  Score 2:  Occasionally — a few positive moments weekly
  Score 3:  Often — positive feelings on most days
  Score 4:  Daily — regularly experience positive emotions
Clinical note: Positive emotional states support healthier food choices, better sleep, and improved immune function — all crucial for chronic condition management.
Q3.  Do you feel your chronic condition (diabetes, obesity, etc.) affects your self-image or confidence?
  Score 1:  Severely — it strongly impacts my self-worth
  Score 2:  Quite a bit — often feel defined by my condition
  Score 3:  Somewhat — occasional self-image concerns
  Score 4:  Minimally — condition does not define me
Clinical note: Negative self-image and health-related stigma are barriers to seeking care and adhering to dietary changes — addressing this is key in clinical nutrition counselling.
Q4.  How often do you eat in response to emotions like boredom, loneliness, sadness, or frustration?
  Score 1:  Very often — emotional eating is a daily pattern
  Score 2:  Often — several times a week
  Score 3:  Sometimes — occasionally eat emotionally
  Score 4:  Rarely — generally eat only when physically hungry
Clinical note: Emotional eating is a major driver of obesity and poor glycaemic control. Identification and intervention by a dietitian is highly effective in managing this.
Q5.  How motivated do you currently feel to make changes to your diet and lifestyle for your health?
  Score 1:  Not motivated — not ready to make changes
  Score 2:  Somewhat motivated — thinking about it
  Score 3:  Motivated — willing but need guidance
  Score 4:  Highly motivated — ready and committed to change
Clinical note: Readiness to change (based on Transtheoretical Model) determines which nutrition intervention strategy to use — from motivational interviewing to structured meal plans.
Scoring Guide & Interpretation
How Scoring Works
Each question has 4 response options scored on a 1–4 scale:
Score
Level
Interpretation
1
Critical / Needs urgent attention
Behaviour or condition is significantly impacting health and requires immediate clinical focus.
2
Below average / Concerning
Some improvement has been made but the behaviour is still below recommended levels for chronic condition management.
3
Moderate / On the right track
Behaviour meets basic health guidelines. Further optimisation is recommended to fully support chronic condition management.
4
Optimal / Keep maintaining
Behaviour is at or above recommended levels. Maintain and use as a strength to support lower-scoring domains.
Domain Score Calculation
Each domain has 5 questions, each scored 1–4.
Raw Domain Score  =  Sum of all 5 question scores  (range: 5–20)
Domain Score (out of 100)  =  (Raw Score / 20) x 100

Using LQS in Clinical Practice
1.  Administer at first consultation as a baseline assessment.
2.  Identify the 1–2 lowest-scoring domains as the primary focus for nutrition counselling.
3.  Re-administer every 8–12 weeks to track progress and adjust the intervention plan.
4.  Use domain scores to guide referrals — low Sleep score may warrant a sleep study; low Emotional Health score may benefit from referral to a psychologist.
5.  Share the score with the patient using the visual dashboard tool to increase engagement and motivation.
This assessment template was created for clinical use by a qualified dietitian.
Always supplement with clinical biochemistry data (HbA1c, BMI, lipid panel) for complete patient assessment.
